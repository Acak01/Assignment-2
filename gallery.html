Shallow Depth Of Field

In this picture of the cat, I used an f/4.5 aperture to isolate the face of the cat with a shallow depth of field. The facial features of the cat, the eyes and whiskers, are in focus, while the objects in the background, like the yellow wall, are in blur. This type of effect accentuates the face of the cat, giving the viewer an unambiguous focus on the subject. The blurred background provides intimacy and brings the focus on the cat in the picture.

Deep Depth Of Field

In this image, I used a setting of f/22 to get a deep depth of field. The wide range of focus allows both the foreground (the buildings) and the background (the sky) to be clear and in focus. The closed-down aperture ensures everything in the scene, from the closest objects to the distant sky, is in focus, resulting in the image appearing more detailed and immersive. This site was chosen to capture the clarity of the entire scenery, from the buildings to the clear sky at sunset.

Stop Motion

To capture this photo of a soaring soccer ball, I used a shutter speed of 1/1000 to freeze the motion of the ball in the air. The fast shutter speed captures the sharp edges of the ball and the blur from moving objects is eliminated. The image's sharpness is what creates the illusion that the ball is suspended in the air without blur or distortion, a dynamic yet static depiction of the action.

Blur Motion

For this image of a car cornering on a night road, I used a 1/10 shutter speed to create motion blur. The slow shutter speed allows the movement of the car to be captured, with the streaked trajectory of the headlights as the car navigates the corner. The background is relatively sharp, but the movement of the car is emphasized, with the car appearing to speed through the scene. This effect adds to the sense of movement and tension in the image.
